Monday, 10th April We woke at 10 and after waiting at the wrong ferry jetty for 15 minutes for the hover-ferry, we caught a bus and train into Tsim Sha Tsui, the area in Kowloon where several travel agents are located. We visited a couple with a list of our inquiries relating to the Trans-Siberian train and also about other alternative methods of getting back to Europe.

Although we had slight hangovers and were not thinking clearly, it soon became apparent that the Trans-Siberian was a far too expensive option and that our best bet would be to go on a three to four week trip around China and then fly back to Bangkok spending five weeks in The Philippines on the way. From Bangkok, we should have more cheap options for getting back to Europe than from Hong Kong.

By the time we had got all the information, we were worn out, still feeling the effects of last night, so decided to call it a day and headed back to Ed's. Ed and Gary got back from work at 7ish and as it was Ed's birthday, we all went out to a local bar where some of his workmates had laid on a surprise party for him. It was a very good night out in an English type bar with pool table and although none of us were drinking much, it was quite entertaining watching some of the others getting progressively more pissed. Ed was given a cake and champagne by his workmates and the evening eventually came to a halt when the bar closed at 2am.

Tuesday, 11th April We woke up too late to make the hover-ferry so after making a few phone calls, we went into Tsim Sha Tsui byt train and bus again. After lunch in McDonalds, we went to a travel agent where Roz and I paid for our tickets to Manila for 18th May. We caught the Star Ferry over to Wan Chai where the three of us left our passports at the Chinese visa office as we have decided to go into China on Friday.

We went back to Ed's by train and bus again (as we still haven't managed to figure out the hover-ferry service) and had a quiet night in the flat.

Wednesday, 12th April We got up and successfully caught the hover-ferry to Wan Chai and went to the visa office to collect our passports complete with Chinese visas. It was a bit cloudy for sight-seeing but we caught a tram to the Mid-Level escalators and spent an hour going up and down. We had a meal in a Chinese restaurant - our first meal in Hong Kong that was not a McDonalds - then Hoggy returned to Ed's while Roz and I crossed on the Star Ferry to Tsim Sha Tsui to collect our tickets for The Philippines and Bangkok. We spent half an hour or so in book shops reading travel guides for ideas for our journey home and then returned to Ed's by train and bus.

We had another quiet evening in.

Thursday, 13th April We didn't do a lot all day apart from a visit to the Tuen Mun shopping centre in the morning. Most of the afternoon was taken up by sorting out what stuff we are taking on our trip into China tomorrow.

In the evening, we finished off the Mekong and had a few beers finally getting to bed at 2ish.

Friday, 14th April We left Ed's flat at 9:30 and went by bus and train to Kowloon Tong railway station where we met Hoggy who had been trying to find new shoes. We arrived at the train station at 10:30 and spent most of the next four hours standing in queues. It appeared as if half of the residents of Hong Kong were traveling to China for the Easter bank holiday (400,000 people we discovered later). We waited about an hour just to buy a ticket to Lo Wu near the Chinese border. We arrived at Lo Wu and stood for about two hours going through customs and immigration before finally walking into China at about 2pm. We walked straight to the railway station and had our first attempt at buying train tickets. We got them without too many problems, the only difficulty being the fact that they would not accept Hong Kong dollars despite what Lonely Planet had said. By the time I got back from the bank, all the seats had been sold on the train we wanted to catch so we ended up buying tickets to stand in the 1st Class section. We had a couple of hours to wait so made another visit to the bank to change more money and also ate a meal in the station.

Our train left on time at 4:45 and was fast and comfortable. It would have been very good if we had had seats as well but we were relieved to be getting on a train at all.

By the time we arrived in Canton, two hours later, we were totally worn out. We tried to find a hostel near the railway station but the one recommended in the book appeared to have closed down. We were eventually found by a tout who took us back to a hostel about four miles away. By the time we had got there and sorted ourselves out, it was gone 8 and within an hour we were all asleep.

Saturday, 15th April We got up at 8 and got a taxi back to Canton railway station. We had decided to leave Canton as soon as possible. The room we stayed in last night was OK but the rest of the hotel left a lot to be desired. It was also quite expensive as we have to pay tourist rates and the rest of Canton did not seem to have a lot to offer. We had the choice of boat or train to leave Canton and decided to get on which ever one we could get tickets for.

We tried first at the CITS, the tourist information service which was as helpful as we had expected and could sell us neither the boat or train tickets. We then tried at the railway station which was totally chaotic. We were sent to a couple of places before finding the right queue. When we reached the front of the queue, a Japanese bloke who spoke Chinese tried to buy tickets for us but was told that there were no hard sleepers (2nd class) left only the soft ones (1st class) costing Â£30 for the 18 hour journey. We decided to give up on the train and dive the boat a try instead. We got another taxi out to the wharf and managed to buy the combination boat and bus ticket that we wanted with remarkable ease. It seems that every backpacker who turns up at the wharf wants exactly the same thing so the woman was able to issue the tickets without us having to say a lot.

We had a couple of hours wait for the boat during which time we got ourselves a meal and I had a haircut.

We got on the boat just after midday and it left on time at 12:30. The boat was a lot more comfortable than we had expected it to be, far better than the Pelni ships of Indonesia. We were each given a numbered bunk in a reasonably comfortable cool dorm, and spent the afternoon reading and playing cards.

We had a pretty good evening meal in the restaurant on the boat and then stayed awake until midnight for the football results.

Sunday, 16th April We were woken at 5:45 when the boat docked at a small town. After some confusion, we established we were not at our stop so we waited on our bunks for an hour and 45 minutes until we arrived at Wuzhou. We had bought bus tickets through to Yangshou and spent half an hour along with four other backpackers trying to find the correct bus. We were eventually helped out by a Chinese girl who turned out to be the bus conductress. She got us seat numbers and showed us to the bus.

According to Lonely Planet, the bus journey should have taken seven hours. Someone at the bus station told us it would take nine and it eventually took about ten. The journey was not too bad but would have been a lot worse if the bus had been full. As it was, we had three seats per two people for the whole journey so it was just about bearable.

We arrived in Yangshou at 6:30 and went straight to a hotel opposite the bus station. Here the prices werre one tenth of what they were in Canton and the hotel was much better. After showers we went out to eat and explore the rest of the town.

We were all very impressed. Yangshou is supposed to be one of the two best backpackers centres in China. There are plenty of good cheap restaurants, travel agents and excellent souvenir shops. We had good meals and several beers all for under Â£1 each. Beer is reasonably good and costs 20p a pint.
