dwjhaines - 2012 week 18

Monday, 24th September We had a few more hours on our balcony before being picked up by the crew from Black Manta at about 11:00. We were driven down to the harbour and taken by zodiac out to where Black Manta was moored. Most of the other guests were already on board. We were shown to our cabin and spent 30 minutes or so unpacking and setting up our gear.

The boat itself is excellent. We have an en-suite cabin with probably as much space as we have had on any live-aboard. The dive deck is huge for the amount of divers on the boat and there is loads of space for sitting around in the dining area and sundeck as well as a large air-conditioned lounge. We had lunch at 12:30 before a long briefing and the first dive at 2pm.

There are about 17 divers on the boat. We were put into a group with Brad (from America) and Jurg (from Switzerland). The group was led by Stanny, an Indonesian guide. The first dive was the check dive at the first dive site we came to out of Labuan Bajo. This was not that good a site and the vis was pretty poor but still enjoyable enough. The second dive at 5:15 was better. This was a sunset dive so it was just about dark by the time we got back out of the water. This site was right on the edge of the Komodo National Marine Park where we will do all the rest of our dives on this trip. It was much better than the first one with quite a lot to see (frog fish, ghost pipe fish, nudi's, a Spanish dancer and a group of bump-head parrot fish).

Back on the boat, dinner was served at 7:30. After an hour or so of sitting around in the dining area, everyone had gone to bed by about 9:45 while the boat continued moving to the Southern Komodo dive sites. So far the trip seems pretty good. The food is excellent and Cedric, the tour leader, seems to have everything pretty well organised. The level of service on the boat is certainly higher than anything we have had before in Asia and is more comparable to the luxury boats in the Red Sea. Our towels get dried for us between each dive and there was hot chocolate handed to us at the end of the last dive!

Tuesday, 25th September First dive of the day was Torpedo Alley, just off Rinca, at 7:30. We had been told that the water would be colder on the Southern Komodo sites so we borrowed full length wet-suits for today's dives. As soon as we hit the water, we realised that this was a very good move!

The water temperature was about 24ยบ which, although only a few degrees cooler than what we are used to, feels a lot, lot colder. Once we were used to the temperature, the dive was excellent. This was proper muck diving again - a black sandy slope covered in strange fish. The highlight was probably a tiny white frog fish but we also saw a juvenile flamboyant cuttle fish, two robust ghost pipe fish, some huge nudi's that we have not seen before, a fingered dragonet and spent about five minutes watching a mimic octopus.

It was back to the boat for breakfast and we then went up to the beach to look at five Komodo Dragons. These huge lizards only exist in this part of the world and we will be doing a walk to see more of them later in this trip. However, we had not expected to be able to see them like this. We were taken by the zodiacs up to where we could see a group of dragons on the beach. Although they are very dangerous, they will not go into cold water, so we could stand in the sea a few metres away from them knowing that they could not get to us.

The second dive of the day at Cannibal Rock was not as good as the first but we did see three turtles. The third dive at Pink Beach was particularly poor. It was still cold and the vis had dropped to five metres or less. After 48 minutes we aborted the dive. It was not worth staying any longer.

The fourth dive of the day was a night dive and was very good. Loads to see - mainly similar sort of stuff to what we saw on the first dive of the day. All in all, a pretty good days diving but it is supposed to get better (and warmer!) tomorrow.

Wednesday, 26th September We had made it up to the Northern Komodo dive sites by this morning meaning that we could go back to wearing shorties. The first dive was at Batu Balong. As soon as we hit the water, you could tell there was a huge improvement on yesterdays conditions. The water was blue, and vis was incomparable to what it had been like yesterday afternoon. While we had been exceptionally unlucky at Pink Beach, I think we were very lucky here with perfect visibility and no current. The dive site was excellent. We saw a few sharks but it was the sheer number of fish around the island that made the dive very enjoyable.

The second dive at Shotgun was also very good. Once again, the currents were no-where near as bad as they can be here and it was a fairly easy dive. We saw more sharks as well as hawksbill turtles and right at the end, a manta ray. We saw it briefly swimming past as we were looking at a turtle and we were then able to watch it at a cleaning station for a minute or two before it swam off.

The third dive was at Castle Rock and we did have some current for the first time. There was an unbelievable number of fish at the start of the dive including schools of giant travallies -  some of the biggest we have ever seen. There was another shark in the middle of all the fish but it was the sheer numbers of fish in all directions that made the dive memorable. This was the first (and probably last) time we had tried using reef hooks - not a great success. They may be useful when you get used to them or if the current is stronger but they seemed more hassle than they were worth on this dive.

We were told by Cedric and Stanny, our guide, that the night dive here would not be very good. They were both right!

Thursday, 27th September First dive of the day was at Crystal Rock, one of Komodo's most famous sites. This was spectacular again and we did manage to make use of our reef hooks with more success than yesterday. On the second dive (Golden Passage) there was quite a lot to see but a lot of the dive was spent photographing pygmy sea-horses which got a bit boring after a while. There were several sharks around and we got a distant glimpse of an eagle ray.

The third dive was back at Castle Rock and was the best dive of the live-aboard so far. At 3:00 we were told that there was very little current and were given the option of waiting for the current to pick up so there would be more to see. We chose to wait and did not go in until 3:55 by which time there was no current at all! This did not spoil the dive. Once again there was a huge number of fish around. We did see sharks again but the large schools of fish swimming round us for most of the dive, particularly the school of fusiliers in the last 10 minutes were really spectacular. The complete lack of current meant we could just stay in one place and watch them all.

The night dive was slightly better than last nights!

Friday, 28th September We started today at Makasar Reef - also known as Mantas on da Moon. The whole site - about a mile long - is at about 9 - 12 metres and the bottom is bare and covered in rubble making it resemble the surface of the moon. It is also a site famous for mantas. We jumped in at one end of the reef and drifted with the current past a series of manta cleaning stations. Our group saw 8 or 9 mantas in total during the dive.

The second dive was at Tatawa Kecil. This is where ReefSekers lost divers a couple of years ago and the current was looking strong as we got ready to dive. However, we managed to stay in the shelter of the island for most of the dive and were never affected by it that much.

This was another excellent dive with loads to see. We dropped down to about 24m to watch three sharks swimming around. When we moved from there we saw a hawksbill turtle. While we were all looking at the turtle, another manta glided over us. This came closer to me than any had on the previous dive. We saw a group of about four or five bump-head parrot fish go past while watching everything else. Because of the amount of time we had spent at 24m, most of the group were getting quite low on air so we spent the last third of the dive in a shallow coral garden. Here there was another hawksbill swimming around which kept Roz entertained for 10 minutes or so.

The third dive was quite a fast drift dive for about 1.5 miles along the side of the island. We did spot a few turtles as we flew along the reef (Roz counted six). The dive ended on a quieter reef with nice coral again and no current. The fourth dive was supposed to be a sunset dive but by the time everyone had decided what they were doing and got themselves ready, it was just about a night dive. It was however lots better than the night dives of the previous two nights! We had decided not to bother with the mandarin fish which Brad and Jurg were keen on seeing so left them at the top of the reef. Within a few minutes, Stanny found a hairy octopus. He was very excited about this and we spent about 20 minutes watching it before moving on. There was loads more to see including another frog fish and a pair of harlequin shrimps. Definitely one of our better night dives.

Saturday, 29th September Our last day of diving on this live-aboard. We did two dives in the morning. Both of these were pretty good without being overly spectacular. On both dives there was very nice coral and plenty of fish but not quite the same amount to see as on some of the previous days dives.

We had expected to do two dives and have a trip to see the Komodo dragons on Rinca in the afternoon but we were given the option of doing a third dive at Wainilu (the location of yesterday's night dive) instead of the dragons. We had been on the dragon trip twelve years ago so took the option of the third dive instead despite the fact that we fly at 10:05 tomorrow morning. This was a good decision as the third dive was one of the best of the trip (and the dragon adventure sounded every bit as boring as it was twelve years ago!). Only four us did the dive - me and Roz together with two of the Asian divers. Everyone else had either come down with some sort of bug or went to see the dragons. I was keen to see the harlequin shrimps again. They had moved from where they were last night but the guides managed to find them again. We also saw, three more frog fish, two ribbon eels, two ornate ghost pipe fish and a sea-horse.

We just about had the boat to ourselves when we got back from the third dive. We were able to wash all our gear and get it hung up to dry while waiting for everyone to return from seeing the dragons. Once everyone was back on board, we returned to the harbour in Labuan Bajo. It was not too lively on the boat - five of the eighteen guests having come down with the bug - so after eating, we went ashore to a "party" taking place in the harbour. Four of us went as well as some of the Indonesian crew. We stayed there for about an hour and were back on the boat by 10. Got to bed after spending an hour or so looking at photos with Stanny.

Sunday, 30th September We were up at 6:30 and had an hour and a half to pack up our equipment and have breakfast before we had to leave. We left the boat via the zodiacs again and then were transferred from the pier to the airport. It was the end of a very good trip. The boat is one of the best dive boats we have ever been on. The diving has been excellent, the food good, and it has been a very good group of people. We are already looking forward to coming back onto the same boat for another trip in November!

Fourteen of us from the trip were on the same Trans Nusa flight back to Bali at 10:30. The check-in was all a bit slow but we all got checked in eventually and the flight left on time. We were back in Bali by about 11:30 and after waiting around for a while for our luggage, were met by a driver to take us to Reef Seen at Permuteran. The journey to Permuteran took about four hours including a couple of photo stops. It was a scenic route straight through the centre of Bali with temples, lakes and mountains to look at on the way. We arrived at Reef Seen just after 4 and were shown to our room. We met up with Amanda who is one of the managers here and who used to swim in Tadley with Roz. We had been carrying an underwater camera housing for her from England so she was pleased to see us! Once again, we have a nice room that just about has a sea view from the balcony. The balcony is also about 10m from the turtle hatchery which has over 400 baby olive ridley turtles and one fully grown hawksbill. Plenty to keep Roz entertained for the next six days!

Again, we were both worn out from both the last week's diving and the travelling today. We spent an hour or so catching up on what has been happening in the world as we had internet access for the first time in a week, and went out to eat at a restaurant just behind where we are staying. We were both asleep just after 9.
